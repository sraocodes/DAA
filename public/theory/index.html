Introduction

Richard's Equation

“## Richard’s Equation Richard’s equation is a widely used mathematical model in soil science and hydrology. It describes how water moves through unsaturated soils. Here is the general form of the equation: $$ \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot (D(\theta) \nabla \theta) - \frac{\partial K(\theta)}{\partial z} $$

Variables in the Equation

  • \(\theta\) is the volumetric water content;
  • \(t\) represents time;
  • \(D(\theta)\) is the soil water diffusivity;
  • \(K(\theta)\) is the hydraulic conductivity;
  • \(z\) is the spatial coordinate in the vertical direction (positive downwards).

The equation is used to calculate the water content at any point in the soil given the diffusivity and hydraulic conductivity as functions of the water content.”

Discretization of Richard's Equation

“## Discretization of Richard’s Equation To discretize Richards’ equation for numerical solution methods, a common approach involves transforming the continuous differential equation into a system of algebraic equations using finite difference or finite element methods. Here’s a detailed breakdown:

Finite Difference Method (FDM)

For spatial discretization, consider a grid with points spaced at \(\Delta x\) and \(\Delta z\) in the horizontal and vertical directions respectively. The derivatives can be approximated using finite differences: $$ \frac{K_{i+1/2,j} (\theta_{i+1,j} - \theta_{i,j})}{\Delta x} - \frac{K_{i-1/2,j} (\theta_{i,j} - \theta_{i-1,j})}{\Delta x} + \frac{K_{i,j+1/2} (\theta_{i,j+1} - \theta_{i,j})}{\Delta z} - \frac{K_{i,j-1/2} (\theta_{i,j} - \theta_{i,j-1})}{\Delta z} $$

Time Discretization

The temporal part of the equation is typically discretized using the backward Euler method for stability, which approximates: $$ \frac{\theta_{i,j}^{n+1} - \theta_{i,j}^n}{\Delta t} $$

Picard Iteration for Linearization

Picard iteration is used to handle non-linearity in \(K(\theta)\). It involves assuming an initial guess for \(\theta\) or \(h\) and iteratively refining this guess. Linearized at each iteration based on previous values: $$ \theta_{i,j}^{n+1,k+1} = \theta_{i,j}^{n} + \Delta t \left( \nabla \cdot (K(\theta_{i,j}^{n+1,k}) \nabla h_{i,j}^{n+1,k}) + S \right) $$

Gauss-Seidel Method

The Gauss-Seidel method sequentially updates each grid point using the most current values, enhancing convergence stability: $$ \theta_{i,j}^{n+1} = \theta_{i,j}^{n} + \Delta t \left( \nabla \cdot (K(\theta_{i,j}^{n+1}) \nabla h_{i,j}^{n+1}) + S \right) $$ "
